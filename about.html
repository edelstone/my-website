How I work

What am I? I’m a designer. Part visual, part experience, part animation, part code. And oh yeah, I write too. In a world of nebulous roles, I think this makes me a product designer – and a pretty decent one. The important thing is this: I enjoy designing and building interfaces that are equal parts useful and beautiful. I make decisions with a reasonable amount of real-world evidence and a strong, mature bias to action.

What sorts of problems do I solve? I’m most-energized working at the nexus of content and design. Building modular components, creating style guides, and writing smart web copy are areas I’ve owned in the past. I like being able to build and prototype what I design, so I’m fluent in much of the front end toolset and have been designing in the browser since 2009. I am constantly learning new things to enrich my personal life and professional craft; currently I’m getting into Sketch, flexbox, and vanilla JavaScript.

Where am I coming from? My social science education introduced me to the writing process and the importance of content organization early on. Years as a professional bicycle mechanic taught me creative problem solving and how to foster lasting customer relationships. I’ve proudly carried these talents forward into my design career. I keep humans at the center of my projects, and believe that words are the foundation of genuine digital experiences.

I’ve worked for some stellar organizations that shared these ideals and helped me build an effective design process around them. Here’s a couple of them:

Texas State University

2014 – 2017

I was the principal designer and front end specialist at Texas State, working across all of the university’s public web properties and ultimately defining their digital brand.

I was directly responsible for the design and prototyping of a responsive, scalable template released to 400+ units in early 2016. I also led the design, front end development and content strategy for the school’s first responsive homepage. These updates gave me the opportunity to implement a much stronger digital brand identity, which I documented in a universal style guide for both local publishers and the general public.

Other notable successes include a mobile-friendly jobs portal, a thoughtful content strategy for the University Marketing website (including a fun team page), improved emergency notifications, a new library homepage, and the growth of a small web team that I helped recruit. My inclusive job announcements got some accolades on Twitter and influenced the culture of several university units.

Plano Cycling

2012 – 2013

As lead designer at Plano Cycling, I proposed and executed digital initiatives on all customer-facing platforms: desktop, mobile, email, and social media.

In March 2013, I designed and deployed a new e-commerce website for the shop, which included a deliberate brand identity and vibrant graphical style. I was also responsible for the new content strategy which balanced core brick-and-mortar services with online shopping experiences in an understandable way. Later I designed and shipped a mobile template that dramatically increased engagement, as measured in increased mobile sales and improved key metrics on customer feedback surveys.

Some other projects I worked on were the design and development of a custom email template, and a social media campaign that raised thousands of dollars in cash and goods for Oklahoma tornado victims.

So, what’s next?

I’m proud of my accomplishments, but I’m certain I’ve only skimmed the surface of my potential. I want to get way better at this design stuff, and build some cool things while I’m at it.

My ideal future team is supportive, talented, realistic, and design-focused, with at least a handful of folks who understand that the 1980s was objectively the best decade ever. I see myself at a small or medium-sized organization with a remote-friendly structure and a culture of trust, autonomy, and ownership. Ideally they have a sophisticated product or business direction, but embrace innovation and experimentation. Shipping design and code updates regularly would be awesome. (Bonus points for employers that match or incentivize charitable contributions).
