Summary

The Texas State homepage and primary template, as well as many other websites, apps, and portals are designed using elements of the digital brand system I conceived. It's publicly documented over at styleguide.txstate.edu.

Context

Texas State's central marketing efforts matured during a time of heavy growth for the print medium in higher education. Major web initiatives had few advocates, and were unable to attract resources or have their impact fully articulated to leadership.

Texas State also underwent two major name changes in a ten-year period, which added disruption and confusion. The brand was often called inconsistent, contentious, or underdeveloped. Most agreed it was print-centric and not sufficiently documented.

Texas State brand documentation in October 2014

Goals

Producing an understandable and scalable digital identity would be vital to the success of planned digital projects, as well as the overall image of the institution. We wanted to:

  • Build a compelling, web-first brand identity
  • Create a systematic and strictly accessible color scheme
  • Devise sensible rules about logo use
  • Bring institutional fonts onto the web
  • Document style resources in a single location

Process

Our digital brand needed to be purposeful, condensed, fluid, and discoverable. We kept this goal in mind over three broad phases:

  1. Theory and strategy
  2. Real-world implementation
  3. Documentation and awareness

Theory and strategy

After auditing existing brand elements and having in-depth discussions with veteran employees, I built a bunch of random demos to see how the colors, logos, and type behaved visually on the screen. I calculated the contrast of existing colors and thoughtfully altered, discarded, or added colors to the palette.

The official Texas State typefaces are Univers and Adobe Garamond Pro. Print designers regularly worked with a over 20 weights of Univers and used Garamond, Garamond Premier, and Adobe Garamond interchangeably. Favoring simplicity and peformance, I narrowed this down to a manageable selection.

Real-world implementation

The digital brand really gained sophistication during the Texas State primary template project, which gave me the opportunity to solicit community feedback and apply styles to living content components.

Several major projects – including some by other departments than my own, and third parties – have brought the digital brand to life since releasing it.

Documentation and awareness

There are hundreds of staff members and vendors that manage digital resources for Texas State audiences, most of whom are untrained in web design or publishing. Documenting our brand strategy and philosophy would keep products consistent, instill a sense of pride and confidence, and ensure continuity when key staff separated from the institution. In addition to my contributions to the brand, I also organized and wrote most of the copy on the site.

The Texas State style guide is public. We consider it a contribution to the higher ed tech and style guide communities that inspired parts of this project.

Major Improvements Over Previous Implementation

  • Institutional fonts brought to web
  • All colors meet AAA accessibility contrast standards
  • Systematic tints and shades
  • Modern patterns added to brand
  • Voice, tone, and photographic style documented
  • Sensible logo rules

Noteworthy Features

Colors

Colors were a challenging part of this project, partly because a large portion of the palette did not meet AAA accessibility standards, including the university's primary gold color. I tested over a dozen gold options before settling on an official Texas State Gold.

I wanted designers to have a robust variety of tints and shades for things like shadows, gradients, and other design subtleties. The university didn't have any approved tints or a reproducible method for deriving them, so I developed a system for this, documented it, and later produced an open-source color generator that mimicked my process. There's a Texas State fork, too.

Tints and shades were derived systematically

Buttons

The university's CMS lacked a button component until recently, so content editors either went without or crafted them in Photoshop (which was inconsistent and kind of an odd necessity). In response, I designed a set of customizable buttons made with HTML and CSS, and featuring some fun interactions. They were released with the Texas State primary template in 2016 and are a major feature of the digital brand.

Is it perfect?

Yes! JK, no. Based on the current position of the university regarding digital priorities, I couldn't have asked for better. But if I could change anything it would be:

  • Not confident in fonts: The Univers font wasn't designed for the web. I favored readability over tradition, but it was more politically expedient to stick with established typography.
  • Typeset logo would be nice: Many universities are including web typeset logos in their digital identities for flexibility and mobile-friendliness. I didn't have approval or time to pursue this, but would've liked to.
  • Needs more texture: Some portions of our digital properties are quite flat due to the lack of background patterns and textures. I see some institutions adding brand textures lately, but I realized this a little too late.
